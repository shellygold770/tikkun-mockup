WHAT DO WE MEAN BY "ALTERNATIVE" HEALING?

“Alternative healing” is not a category of last resort — and it is not only about peak or intensive experiences. For many people it looks like IFS, breathwork workshops, or somatic therapy: approaches that deepen or complement conventional care they already have. For others it means something more intensive. The path depends entirely on the person, their history, and where they are right now.

What these approaches share is that they work with dimensions of human experience — body, depth, and spirit — that standard talk therapy or medication often cannot reach on their own. They are not replacements for conventional care. They are complements, expansions, and in some cases the missing piece that allows the conventional work to finally land.

The evidence base is growing rapidly. What was once considered fringe is now the subject of serious clinical research at major institutions. The question is no longer whether these approaches work. The question is how to pursue them safely, responsibly, and with integrity — which is exactly what we are built to answer.

That question — how to pursue genuine healing without compromising clinical safety or spiritual integrity — is exactly what Tikkun HaNefesh Foundation was built to answer for the Jewish community.

This Is the Problem We Exist to Solve

In 1979, the Rebbe identified exactly what Jewish individuals seeking alternative healing face: spaces where genuine therapeutic benefit is embedded within spiritual frameworks incompatible with Jewish faith.

The Rebbe's position was not that alternative healing should be avoided — it was that a structure should be built so that it could be pursued without spiritual compromise.

Tikkun HaNefesh Foundation applies this principle to the full spectrum of alternative healing — not only meditation, but every modality that carries therapeutic benefit alongside spiritual risk.

These are not separate opinions. They are one tradition, speaking across time — affirming that healing and faith are not in conflict, and that the Jewish community deserves a path that honors both.

Our Halachic Vetting Standard

WHAT WE ACTUALLY CHECK FOR.

Every practitioner, facilitator, and healer in our network is evaluated against three specific principles of Jewish law.

Hashem Echad

G-d is One

Judaism maintains absolute monotheism. All facilitation language must be monotheistically neutral. No invocation of directional forces, spirits, or independent spiritual beings of any kind — even casually or symbolically.

In practice: No Four Directions, no Grandfather Spirit, no Mother Earth as an independent power. All language must reflect the singular Creator.

K'garzen Beyad
Hachotzeiv

"Like an axe in the hand of the woodcutter"

Everything — including elements used in healing — is an instrument of the one Creator. Stones, fire, steam, water, and breath have no independent spiritual agency. They are tools, not beings.

In practice: No stones that "hold spirits," fire that "teaches," or ancestors that "guide directly." Objects are instruments — not sources of independent power.

Lo Tizkor

No invoking foreign deity names

Jewish law prohibits naming or invoking foreign gods, sacred beings, or personified spiritual forces — even casually or symbolically. This includes named spirits, tribal deities, and invocation songs from other traditions.

In practice: Any ceremony element, song, prayer, or invocation with a named deity or sacred being from another tradition must be removed or adapted.

Why This Network Exists

THE COMMUNITY
NEEDS YOU TO
GET THIS RIGHT.

Observant Jews are seeking alternative healing in growing numbers — not because they are chasing trends, but because conventional medicine and standard therapy have not been enough. Trauma that hasn't resolved. Grief that hasn't moved. Patterns that haven't broken. The need is real, it is serious, and it is growing.

The Clinical Dimension

Alternative healing works — when it is done well. Somatic approaches, breathwork, depth processes, and medicine work have demonstrated real therapeutic outcomes for trauma, depression, end-of-life distress, treatment-resistant conditions, and more. The evidence base is building. The clinical need is not speculative.

But the space is largely unregulated. Many practitioners operate without adequate training, no crisis protocols, no supervision, no integration frameworks. The person seeking genuine healing can be seriously harmed — not through spiritual compromise, but through clinical negligence. Clinical excellence is not optional in this work. It is the baseline.

And there is a specific failure pattern we have encountered repeatedly — even among healers who are culturally sensitive, who understand the community, who would never compromise on the spiritual dimension. They skip the screening. They skip the preparation. They take people through an experience and consider the work done. No integration. No follow-through. An experience without a container is not healing. It is exposure. And for many people — particularly those carrying significant trauma — it can leave them worse off than before.

This is what the four-phase model exists to prevent. Intake. Preparation. Experience. Integration. Every participant moves through all four. Every healer on our list understands that the experience is one phase — not the product.

The Spiritual Dimension

And then there is the second problem — specific to this community. The alternative healing world embeds real therapeutic benefit inside spiritual containers that raise serious questions of Avodah Zarah. Directional spirits are invoked. Sacred beings from other traditions are named. A person seeking genuine healing absorbs something they cannot integrate within their emunah or bring to their Rav without shame.

Our Mission

TIKKUN HANEFESH —
REPAIR OF THE SOUL.

The name says everything. Tikkun HaNefesh — repair of the soul — is not a metaphor for us. It is the operational framework for everything we do.

We exist because the Jewish community has been underserved in the space of alternative healing. Crisis resources exist. Therapy referrals exist. What has been missing is a structured, Torah-aligned path for the person who is not in crisis — but who knows that something deeper needs to change.

We are building that path. One participant at a time.

The specific problem we exist to solve has three dimensions.

The first is spiritual: when observant Jews enter general alternative healing spaces, they frequently encounter practitioners who — without malicious intent — embed healing within spiritual frameworks that raise serious questions of Avodah Zarah. Directional spirits are invoked. Sacred beings from other traditions are named. Healing elements are presented as spiritually autonomous. The person seeking genuine healing absorbs something they cannot integrate within their emunah or bring to their Rav without shame.

The second is clinical: the alternative healing world is largely unregulated. Many practitioners operate without adequate training, supervision, crisis protocols, or integration frameworks. A person seeking genuine healing can be seriously harmed — not through spiritual compromise, but through clinical negligence.

The Third Dimension — The Container

The third is structural. Even when someone finds a practitioner who is clinically sound and spiritually aligned, the work often still goes nowhere — because there is no container. They have a powerful experience. Something shifts. And then, with no integration after, the insight dissolves. They seek out the next healer, the next experience, the next modality. They bounce. Not because the healing wasn’t real — but because nothing held it. What they needed was not a better healer. They needed a structure that holds the whole process from first question through lasting change. That structure is what Tikkun HaNefesh Foundation is.

Tikkun HaNefesh Foundation addresses both. We vet every practitioner for halachic sensitivity and clinical competence. We do not ask participants to choose between their faith and their safety — because we refuse to make that choice necessary.

Our Founding Story

WHY WE
BUILT THIS.

We didn't come to this work from the outside. Both of us came through it.

Moishe grew up in Crown Heights, steeped in Chabad tradition — a world with a deep sense of mission and spiritual seriousness. But his path took him through his own trials, including a battle with addiction that became the entry point into understanding healing not as a theory, but as something you have to live through. That personal reckoning led him into nearly two decades of professional work in addiction recovery and mental health — helping treatment centers across the country build ethical, effective systems. He saw what works. He saw what's missing. He understood, from the inside, what it means to build a container that actually holds people.

Aaron's journey carried a similar weight. He struggled with addiction, with anxiety and depression, with years of searching through conventional paths that didn't reach the thing that needed to be reached. He found his way through, and then dedicated himself to holding that same space for others — bringing the same seriousness, the same belief in safety as the foundation of all genuine healing, the same conviction that the inner work cannot be bypassed or rushed.

"We both know what it means to be in a space that doesn't hold you — and what it means to finally be in one that does. That knowledge is what Tikkun HaNefesh Foundation is built on."

Between the two of us, we have spent years inside the alternative healing world — not observing it, but working within it. And what we've seen is something that needs to be named directly: there are facilitators operating in these spaces who are not equipped for the people they are serving. Not equipped clinically. Not equipped spiritually. Not equipped to understand the specific weight carried by someone from an observant Jewish background who walks into a ceremony space and encounters frameworks completely foreign to their emunah.

We've encountered facilitators like that ourselves. We've heard from community members — rabbis, therapists, families — about what happens when someone seeks healing in a space that wasn't built for them. The stories are not abstractions. They are specific, they are painful, and they are preventable.

But there is a third problem — one that exists even within religious and Jewish-adjacent healing spaces. There are healers who understand the community. Who are respectful of Torah values. Who would never invoke a foreign spirit. And who still do not screen properly, do not prepare people, do not follow through on integration. They take people through the experience and consider the work done. That is not healing. That is an event. And in many cases, an experience without preparation or integration is not just incomplete — it can cause real harm, destabilizing people who had no foundation to hold what arose.

This is precisely what the four-phase model is built to address. Intake. Preparation. Experience. Integration. Not as a framework on paper, but as a lived commitment — a structure that holds a person before, during, and after. We built it because we've seen too many people walk away from powerful experiences with nowhere to put what happened to them.

"The difference between the people who transform and the people who don't is not the experience. It is how they came into it — and what happened after."

This is not a theory. It is an observation, made repeatedly, by two people who have spent years inside this space. Two people can enter the same experience with the same facilitator and have completely different outcomes. The one who transforms came in prepared — with intention set, nervous system steadied, inner work already begun. The one who doesn't came in cold, and walked out alone. Preparation and integration are not services added around the experience. They are the work itself.

Tikkun HaNefesh Foundation is the answer we kept wishing existed. A structured path built by people who understand both the transformative potential of this work and the real risks when it is done without rigor — spiritually, clinically, or structurally. A bridge, held by people who have walked both sides of it.

We built this because we've seen what's at stake. And because we believe the Jewish community deserves better than what currently exists.
